Lecture 2:Keaton

The Fred Ott Principle, continued

  • Three phases of media evolution
  • the sociological, technological, artistic, and economic changes that allow the development from the Fred Ott film to artistic, advanced films
  • 1915: Birth of a Nation
    • first full-feature film
    • deeply influenced by the best-selling novel "The Klansman"
  • The myth of technological determinism
    • Technology doesn't necessarily reach its full potential immediately.
    • All new technologies have latencies.
    • (ex) There was always a collection of people going to a theater, watching film produced by a technological elite. No reason it worked out this way.
    • The process of the discovery of the technology is important. However, the technology alone does not explain this process.
    • Technology does not explain why a particular technology becomes an embedded social form.
    • Principle of obsolescence built into the system, too.
  • Often have a cultural limitation
    • The inherited prejudices, diseases, and lies of a society are carried by the technology
    • promulgated a mythology of America
  • Early films show the discovery of the language of film
    • can see a language/syntax for this new visual medium
  • Go from being a nickelodeon/strip show delivery to an embedded social form.
    • mobilize most of the country into a habitual routine.
    • affects the resources available to the medium
    • can rely on previous film knowledge.
      • establishment of a star system (famous movie stars)
      • fundamental movie genres emerge (westerns, urban crime narrative, satire and comedy, etc)
      • genres are useful to a mass-production-style studio system
      • economies of scale are possible as the system begins to stabilize.
  • A paradox: capitalism and the movies
    • capitalist greed was the enabling system for this form of visual art
    • capitalism: crassest of alliances between greed and ?

The Great Train Robbery (1903)

  • Already a refined text, even though it looks like a simple film.
  • Discovers that the shot is the basic unit of action instead of the scene.
    • theatrical style: set up camera on a tripod. actors come on and off.
    • camera becomes mobile. starts moving.
  • Recognition of editing and cutting shots.
    • Recognition of film as a two-stage process: filming and editing.
    • Fast cuts back and forth between scenes: principle of parallel action in Beast at Bay
  • Process shots
    • Some scenes are back-projected, etc.
    • Manipulating the image in illegitimate ways.

Lonedale Operator

  • scripting
  • pre-shot schedule
  • out-of-order filming
    • expensive to set up sets if they appear again later in the movie
  • film deals with hierarchies of gender
    • woman takes over her father's job of a train station as he is ill
    • Shows an anxiety over gender roles that reflects the social gender norms.
  • Repeats the same 3-shot trajectory
    • Has a kind of visual coherence
    • Sets up the structural geography of the film.

Buster Keaton (1895-1966)

  • These jokes could not have been possible in theater.
  • Acrobat / Actor
    • Grew up as a daring acrobat, often injured as a child and in the theater.
    • Discovered that the audience reacted more when his face was expressionless.
    • Called the Great Stoneface
  • Technician / Director
    • Moves from a single joke, to a sequence of jokes, to a narrative
  • Metaphysician / Artist
    • Often the only character in his films.
    • Universe he projects has a content or meaning that exploit his intelligence in creating this universe.

The General (1927)

  • A camera following the train, and Keaton in the film
  • This sequence is a sublimely rich example of the multiplicity principle
  • Scene when woman gets up from bathtub: we're about to see her nude!
    • Film suddenly cuts away. Who did that? Whose hand was that?

Multiplicity Principle

  • At some point, you get to a place where something that starts out as a gag becomes so meaningful that it understands some meaning of life.
  • That is, Keaton's gag films ended up becoming so complicated and sophisticated that it becomes art.
  • When events in the film do more than one job: when they forward the story and are funny and develop the character.
  • Soap operas are often bad at this: I want to give information to the audience, but I end up violating the consistency of the character.

"A culminating text"

  • The General can be understood as a kind of culminating text.
  • Culmination of Keaton's career
    • Basic character is Keaton film's basic character.
    • A steadfast, relentless, muddling character who does everything he can to fight against incredible odds, almost despite himself.
  • Culmination of silent film-making as a whole
    • Keaton expected his audience to recognize his films as a parody of DW Griffith's martial pretentiousness.
    • Also hearkens back to The Great Train Robbery.
    • Features of The General allude to the history of silent film.

The Keaton Universe: Contingency

  • We get through life not because of anything we've done, but because of accident. We live in a contingent universe.
  • Keaton's universe shows us a universe where human action matters: if this human did not keep on so steadfastly, he wouldn't servive. However, his actions don't matter until some accident happens to keep him going.
  • While the universe may screw you up sometimes, it doesn't always. The universe isn't angry; it's just unaware.
  • Shows both the importance and inadequacy of human agency.

The General's Structure

  • The General starts with him going up the track, having a series of adventures. He then goes down the track, repeating a mirrored version of those adventures.

Keaton, once more

  • The General: his masterpiece
  • historical realism
    • location filming was revolutionary
    • remarkable how realistic and accurate his film was
    • patterned scenes after Civil War photographs
    • props, locomotives, etc are all real
  • This historical realism was meant to mock the pretentious martial attitude of movies of that era.
  • Technical achievement of the locomotive crashing into the ravine: can only do once
